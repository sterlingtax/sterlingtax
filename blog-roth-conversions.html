Roth conversions, explained simply

September 20264 min read

Roth conversions come up a lot as people near retirement, and they are often explained in a way that makes them sound more complicated than they are. Here is the plain version.

What it actually is

A Roth conversion means moving money from a traditional retirement account, where withdrawals are taxed later, into a Roth account, where qualified withdrawals come out tax-free. You pay tax on the amount you move now, in exchange for not paying tax on it, or its growth, down the road.

Why anyone would do that

The idea is simple. If you expect to be in a similar or higher tax bracket later, paying the tax now at a known rate can beat paying an unknown rate later. It can also lower the required withdrawals you would otherwise face, and leave a tax-free inheritance for your family.

When it tends to make sense

Conversions often fit best in the lower-income years that show up between leaving work and starting Social Security or required withdrawals. In those years there can be room to convert at a lower rate. The window does not last forever, which is why timing matters.

The catch to watch

A conversion adds to your taxable income for the year, and that can ripple into other things, like the cost of Medicare or how much of your Social Security is taxed. Done carefully and in the right amount, it is a strong tool. Done without a plan, it can create a surprise.
